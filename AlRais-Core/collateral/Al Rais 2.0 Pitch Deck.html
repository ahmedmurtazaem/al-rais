Section two · The decision the build required

None of the SOW's features work well when the platform is locked to one supplier.

Recommendation engine A recommendation engine that only sees one airline's inventory cannot meaningfully recommend.
Package builder Pulling flight, hotel and activity from the same source isn't really a package. It's a bundle.
Chatbot & commercial rules Both inherit the limitations of whichever supplier they happen to depend on.
The answer
A layer that speaks to many suppliers through one interface — and serves as the foundation for every AI-powered feature.

That layer is the middleware. The rest of this brief is about what it does, how it does it, and what the business gets from it.

Al Rais 2.0 · Architecture 06 / 27
What the middleware does

Five things, each one serving the SOW's commercial vision directly.

01

Every supplier, interchangeable.

Provesio today; Sabre, Amadeus, Hotelbeds, Duffel or Viator tomorrow — all behind one consistent interface.

02

A live competition on every search.

Every supplier queried in parallel. Duplicates removed. The customer sees the best offer; Al Rais sees the margin.

03

Commercial rules, applied once.

Markups, blackouts, currency, residence-based pricing, yield — set once in the admin panel, respected everywhere.

04

Multi-supplier packages, held together.

Three components, three suppliers, one transaction — with safe rollback if any single component fails.

05

Failure, contained.

If one supplier's API goes down, the others keep serving. Customers never see an error screen the platform could prevent.

Five

Failure, contained.

If one supplier's API goes down, the platform keeps serving the others. Customers don't see error screens — they see the offers from suppliers still responding.

Per-supplier circuit breakers track failures, open the circuit, and quietly retry until the supplier is healthy again. The platform never depends entirely on any single external system.

Section five · Designed for the GCC traveller

Built with the regional traveller in mind, not the global average.

Visa intelligence, surfaced inline.

Saudi passport holders flying to Malaysia. UAE residents flying to Georgia. Pakistani passport holders flying through Kuala Lumpur. Each one receives accurate context as a small badge on the result card — not buried three pages into the booking flow.

Family-first defaults.

UAE and Saudi travel skews heavily family. The scoring rules treat two adults and two children as a first-class scenario, not a deviation from the solo-traveller default — surfacing carriers and fares that respect this profile without requiring the customer to filter for them.

Honest commercial framing.

Suppliers are named transparently. Where a fare excludes baggage or seat selection, the exclusion is flagged before the customer commits — not discovered after payment. The platform respects the customer's time and trust, the qualities that distinguish a UAE travel brand from a commodity aggregator.

Section six · The chatbot, the recommendation engine, the package builder

The SOW's three AI-powered features are not standalone. They're interfaces to the same intelligence.

Feature one

The chatbot.

Five specialised agents — flight, hotel, activity, package, recommendation — each with a full toolset. Built on LangGraph. Every supplier added to the platform expands what the chatbot can do, without changing the chatbot itself.

Feature two

The recommendation engine.

Curation rules plus a co-occurrence layer that learns from booking history. The longer the platform runs, the better recommendations get. Cross-sell triggers fire at meaningful points — after a flight booking, before checkout, on return-to-site within a day.

Feature three

The dynamic package builder.

The composition engine assembles a holiday from independent suppliers as if it were a single offer. Pricing calculated as a bundle. Cancellation terms aggregated. The customer sees one holiday; the platform manages the underlying complexity.

Feature two · The recommendation engine

Seven weighted dimensions. Plus everything the platform has ever booked.

Curation rules score every offer across seven dimensions. A co-occurrence layer learns from past bookings: travellers who chose A often also chose B. Together, they make the platform smarter every month it runs.

Cross-sell triggers fire at meaningful points — after a flight booking, just before checkout, when the customer revisits within 24 hours. Each one with appropriate context, not a "you might also like" banner.
